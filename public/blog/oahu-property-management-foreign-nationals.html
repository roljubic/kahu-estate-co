O'ahu is one of the world's most sought-after second-home and investment property destinations for foreign nationals — particularly buyers from Japan, Canada, Australia, Germany, and the United Kingdom. Hawaii's stable property values, year-round climate, and unique position as a US jurisdiction with Pacific character make it a compelling combination for international investors.

But owning property in Hawaii as a foreign national is materially more complex than domestic ownership. The tax obligations, withholding requirements, management structures, and compliance landscape are distinct — and the consequences of getting them wrong range from significant financial penalties to deals that fall apart at closing.

This guide covers what international property owners on O'ahu actually need to understand — not a surface-level summary, but the specific rules that affect your investment, your tax position, and your day-to-day property management.

Can Foreign Nationals Own Property in Hawaii?

Yes, without restriction. Hawaii does not have foreign ownership caps, special purchase requirements, or waiting periods for international buyers. A foreign national can purchase fee simple residential or commercial real estate on O'ahu in exactly the same way a US citizen can.

The purchase process is standard: offer, escrow, title search, closing. Foreign buyers typically work with a Hawaii real estate attorney in addition to a buyer's agent — particularly for structuring ownership correctly (individual, LLC, trust) from a tax and estate planning perspective before closing.

Ownership structure matters: How a foreign national holds title to O'ahu property has significant tax and estate implications. Individual ownership, a US LLC, a foreign corporation, and a Hawaii trust all have different FIRPTA, estate tax, and income tax consequences. Consult a Hawaii real estate attorney and a US tax advisor before purchasing — not after.

FIRPTA & HARPTA: The Tax Withholding Rules

This is the area where foreign property owners are most frequently surprised — sometimes at the closing table of a future sale.

FIRPTA (Federal)

The Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act (FIRPTA) requires buyers of US real property from foreign persons to withhold 15% of the gross sales price and remit it to the IRS. Note: this is 15% of the sales price, not the gain. On a $2M property sale, that's $300,000 withheld at closing — regardless of what you paid for the property or what your actual gain is.

The withheld amount is applied against the foreign seller's actual US tax liability when they file a US non-resident income tax return (Form 1040-NR). If the actual tax owed is less than the withholding, the difference is refunded — but this can take months.

FIRPTA withholding can be reduced or eliminated in advance by filing Form 8288-B (Application for Withholding Certificate) before or during the sale. If approved, the IRS issues a withholding certificate specifying the reduced amount. This requires a US tax advisor and planning ahead.

HARPTA (Hawaii State)

Hawaii's state equivalent — the Hawaii Real Property Tax Act — requires an additional 7.25% withholding of the gross sales price, remitted to the Hawaii Department of Taxation. Combined with FIRPTA, a foreign seller faces up to 22.25% withholding on the gross sales price at closing.

The cash flow trap: If you purchased your O'ahu property for $1.5M and are selling for $2M, your actual capital gain might be $350,000 (after costs). But FIRPTA + HARPTA could withhold $445,000 from your sale proceeds at closing. You'll receive the overage as a refund after filing returns — but that reconciliation takes months. Plan accordingly.

FIRPTA Exceptions

  • Sales price is $300,000 or less AND the buyer intends to use the property as a residence → FIRPTA may not apply
  • Seller obtains a withholding certificate from the IRS in advance
  • The property is transferred in certain non-sale transactions

Property Tax for Non-Resident Foreign Owners

All property owners in Hawaii — regardless of nationality — pay annual property taxes to the City and County of Honolulu. For foreign nationals, the critical issue is tax classification.

A foreign national who does not establish Hawaii residency cannot qualify for the Owner-Occupant Homeowner rate. On a $3M Kahala estate, the Residential A classification results in property taxes of approximately $25,000–$30,000/year — versus approximately $6,650/year for an owner-occupant with the Homeowner exemption. The difference ($18,000–$23,000/year) is a significant carrying cost that must be factored into underwriting any O'ahu property investment.

Key deadline: The Homeowner exemption must be filed by December 31 of the year preceding the tax year. For foreign nationals who later establish Hawaii residency, this deadline is critical. Applications filed after December 31 do not apply until the following tax year.

Taxing Rental Income from Abroad

Foreign nationals who rent their O'ahu property must navigate a multi-layer tax compliance framework:

Federal Income Tax on Rental Income

Rental income from US property earned by a foreign person is subject to US income tax. The treatment depends on an election the foreign owner makes:

  • 30% withholding on gross rents (default if no election made) — the tenant or management company is required to withhold 30% of gross rental payments and remit to the IRS
  • Net income election (Form W-8ECI) — the foreign owner can elect to be treated as if engaged in a US trade or business, paying tax on net rental income (after expenses) at ordinary US tax rates. This is usually more favorable and requires filing a US Form 1040-NR annually

Hawaii State Tax on Rental Income

Non-resident property owners earning rental income in Hawaii must file Hawaii Form N-15 (non-resident income tax return) annually. Hawaii taxes rental income at rates up to 11%.

Hawaii GET on Rental Income

Rental income from Hawaii property is subject to the General Excise Tax (GET) — 4.5% in Honolulu. This applies to both short-term and long-term residential rentals, though the GET on long-term rentals (12+ months) is a reduced 0.5%.

TAT for Short-Term Rentals

Short-term rentals (under 30 days) are additionally subject to Hawaii's Transient Accommodations Tax (TAT) at 10.25% — on top of GET. STR owners must register with the Hawaii Department of Taxation and file TAT returns. Platforms like Airbnb collect and remit GET and TAT on behalf of hosts in some cases — verify current status with your tax advisor.

Management Structure Options for Foreign Nationals

Foreign nationals have three primary options for managing their O'ahu property:

Option 1: Self-Management (Not Recommended for Foreign Nationals)

Theoretically possible, but practically unworkable from abroad. Managing a property from another country — different time zone, without local vendor relationships, without the ability to physically respond to emergencies — consistently results in deferred maintenance, unresponsive contractors, and significantly higher long-term costs. Most foreign nationals who attempt self-management eventually transition to professional management after an expensive lesson.

Option 2: Licensed Vacation Rental Management (For STR Properties)

If the property is legally permitted for short-term rentals, a licensed Hawaii real estate broker/property manager can list, manage, and collect revenue on the owner's behalf. This requires a Hawaii real estate license. The management company handles all guest communications, cleaning, maintenance coordination, pricing, and tax remittance (GET/TAT). Typical fee: 20–30% of gross rental revenue.

Option 3: Estate Management (For Primary/Vacation Residences)

For properties that are not rented or that serve as private vacation residences, an estate management service like Kahu Estate Co. provides the local stewardship layer. This is distinct from vacation rental management and does not require a real estate license — it covers property maintenance, vendor management, inspections, and owner liaison.

Many foreign nationals use both: estate management for year-round stewardship, and a licensed rental manager for periods when the property is made available for rent. Kahu Estate Co. coordinates with rental managers and vacation rental companies as part of the integrated service.

Banking & Payments from Overseas

Foreign nationals owning O'ahu property need a mechanism for paying property taxes, utilities, insurance, management fees, and any maintenance costs. Options:

  • US bank account: Opening a US personal or business bank account as a foreign national requires in-person verification at most major banks. Some Hawaii branches of Bank of Hawaii and First Hawaiian Bank have experience with international clients and can facilitate account opening during property closing visits.
  • Wire transfers: Regular wire transfers from a foreign account to pay US-based expenses works but incurs fees ($25–$50 per transfer) and currency conversion costs.
  • Estate management escrow account: Kahu Estate Co. maintains a client funds account for each property. Property owners fund this account quarterly or annually; we pay all O'ahu expenses (property taxes, utilities, vendors, insurance) from it and provide monthly reconciliation statements. This is the simplest approach for foreign nationals.

Currency & Transfer Considerations

Foreign nationals paying O'ahu expenses in USD from foreign currency face exchange rate exposure. A property with $50,000/year in carrying costs fluctuates meaningfully in equivalent JPY, CAD, AUD, or EUR depending on exchange rate movements.

Practical considerations:

  • Wire transfers via services like Wise (formerly TransferWise) or OFX typically offer 1–2% better exchange rates than bank wire transfers
  • Maintaining a USD float in a US account (enough to cover 3–6 months of expenses) reduces the need for frequent transfers and limits exposure to short-term rate volatility
  • Japanese buyers in particular should be aware that BOJ policy and USD/JPY movements have meaningfully changed O'ahu carrying costs over recent years — factor multi-year currency scenarios into property underwriting

Frequently Asked Questions

Can foreign nationals own property in Hawaii?

Yes, without restriction. Foreign nationals can purchase and own Hawaii real estate exactly as US citizens can. The differences are in tax treatment at sale (FIRPTA/HARPTA withholding) and annual property tax classification.

What is HARPTA and how does it affect foreign property owners in Hawaii?

HARPTA requires buyers to withhold 7.25% of the gross sales price when purchasing from a foreign seller, remitted to the Hawaii DOT. Combined with the federal FIRPTA (15%), total withholding can reach 22.25% of the sales price at closing — reconciled after the foreign seller files a Hawaii non-resident tax return.

Do foreign nationals pay property taxes in Hawaii?

Yes. All O'ahu property owners pay property taxes. Foreign nationals are taxed at the Non-Owner Occupant Residential rate (~$4.50–$10.50/$1,000 depending on value) rather than the much lower Owner-Occupant rate. On a $2M+ property, this difference can exceed $20,000/year.

How can foreign nationals ensure their O'ahu property is properly managed?

A local estate management company provides weekly inspections, vendor management, emergency response, utility and tax payment coordination, and a single point of contact — eliminating the practical impossibility of managing O'ahu property from abroad.

Does a foreign national need a Hawaii real estate license to rent out their property?

No — an owner can rent their own property without a license. However, any third-party management company that collects rent on the owner's behalf must be licensed by Hawaii DCCA. STR properties also require a valid Honolulu DPP permit and Hawaii TAT/GET registration.
