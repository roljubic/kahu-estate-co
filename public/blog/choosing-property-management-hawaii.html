Choosing the Right Property Management Company in Hawaii: What to Ask

February 2026  ¬∑  10 min read

There are dozens of property management companies on Oahu. Some are excellent. Most are mediocre. A few will cost you more in deferred maintenance, vendor markups, and missed problems than you'd ever pay in fees.

The stakes are higher in Hawaii than most markets. Your property is in a humid, salt-air environment that's thousands of miles from you. Your manager is your eyes, your voice, and your first line of defense. Getting this decision wrong is expensive.

Here's the framework to get it right.

First: Understand What Type of Manager You Actually Need

Not all property management is the same. There are three distinct categories:

  • Vacation Rental Managers ‚Äî focused on short-term rentals (Airbnb/VRBO). Revenue optimization, guest communications, turnover cleaning. Requires a Hawaii real estate license.
  • Long-Term Rental Managers ‚Äî tenant placement, lease management, maintenance coordination for annual rentals. Also requires a Hawaii real estate license.
  • Estate/Property Stewardship Managers ‚Äî focused on property preservation, vendor oversight, inspections, and owner-focused reporting for homes that aren't actively rented. Different licensing landscape ‚Äî verify credentials.

If you hire a vacation rental manager to steward an estate, or an estate manager who lacks relationships with the right vendors, you're mismatched from day one.

The Interview: 12 Questions That Separate Good Managers from Great Ones

1. How many properties are you currently managing?

You want this number to feel sustainable. A solo operator managing 50+ properties can't give your estate real attention. A boutique firm managing 10‚Äì20 premium properties can.

2. What does a standard weekly inspection look like ‚Äî and what does the report include?

Great managers have a written checklist and deliver photo-documented reports. If they talk vaguely about "checking in," keep looking.

3. How do you handle vendor relationships ‚Äî do you markup vendor invoices?

Some managers take 15‚Äì30% markups on all vendor work without disclosing it. Ask explicitly. Good managers charge a transparent management fee and pass vendor costs through at cost (or at negotiated rates they share with you).

4. What's your emergency response protocol? Give me a specific example.

You want to hear a real story ‚Äî a burst pipe at 2 AM, a hurricane warning, an unauthorized person on the property. What happened? Who called who? How fast? What was resolved?

5. What are your preferred vendors for HVAC, plumbing, roofing, and pest control?

Good managers have licensed, pre-vetted, pre-agreed-rate relationships with key trades. If they name vendors and can speak to their quality, that's a green flag. If they're vague, they're likely figuring it out per-incident ‚Äî which costs you money.

6. How do you communicate with owners, and how often?

You want structured reporting: a weekly or monthly written summary, plus immediate alerts for anything above a defined threshold. "We're available if you call" is not a communication plan.

7. What's your approval process for maintenance and repairs?

There should be a clear threshold: under $X, they handle it; above $X, they get your sign-off. Surprises on your invoice mean the approval protocol doesn't exist or isn't respected.

8. Are you ‚Äî or is your firm ‚Äî licensed by the Hawaii Department of Commerce and Consumer Affairs?

Depending on what services they offer, they may need a Hawaii real estate broker's license. Ask directly. Check the DCCA database yourself.

9. What insurance do you carry?

They should carry General Liability and Errors & Omissions (E&O) insurance. Ask for a Certificate of Insurance. Vendors entering your property should also be insured ‚Äî does your manager verify this?

10. Can you walk me through a situation where something went wrong on a client's property?

You want honesty and competence. Everyone has a "something went wrong" story. What matters is how they handled it, how they communicated it to the owner, and what systems they put in place afterward.

11. What's your termination policy?

If things don't work out, how do you exit? Is there a penalty? How are keys, access codes, and vendor relationships handed off? A manager who makes it hard to leave is a red flag.

12. Can I speak with two or three current clients ‚Äî specifically absentee owners?

Any good manager will say yes immediately. Hesitation means something.

What the Fee Structure Should Look Like

For estate management (non-rental), expect:

  • Monthly retainer: $1,500‚Äì$8,000 depending on property size and scope of services
  • Vendor invoices passed through at cost (or at pre-negotiated rates shared transparently)
  • No hidden markups or "administrative fees" on top of vendor bills
  • Any additional services (event prep, seasonal deep clean coordination) quoted separately in advance

The cheapest manager is never the right manager for a luxury property. What you're paying for is judgment, relationships, and accountability ‚Äî not a body that shows up once a month and sends a bill.

The Decision

After your interviews, you should be evaluating three things:

  1. Trust ‚Äî Do you believe this person will act in your interest when you're not watching? Because you won't be.
  2. Systems ‚Äî Do they have documented processes, or are they improvising? Improvisation at $5M of real estate is unacceptable.
  3. Fit ‚Äî Do they understand your property, your standards, and what "well-managed" looks like to you? A mismatch in expectations is the root of most bad management relationships.
