Why This Matters More on O'ahu Than Anywhere Else

Owning property on O'ahu as a mainland or international resident is different from owning a vacation cabin in Colorado or a condo in Florida. The combination of factors — salt air corrosion, trade wind-driven rain, intense humidity, tropical pest pressure, and the sheer distance from your property — means that problems compound faster and cost more when they aren't caught early.

A burst supply line in Kailua that gets reported to a mainland owner at 6 AM HST (9 AM Pacific) might not see a plumber until afternoon. By then, Hawaii's humidity has already started working against you. A similar situation in Kahala with a professional estate manager on-site? The water is shut off within 20 minutes, photos are documented, and the insurance claim is opened before you've finished your morning coffee.

The emergency contact list below isn't just a nice-to-have. For absentee owners — whether you're in California, Texas, New York, or Tokyo — it's the difference between a $3,000 repair and a $40,000 remediation.

We've organized this by category. At the end, there's a printable template you can fill in with your specific vendors and store somewhere accessible (not just your phone — phones get lost).

Utility Emergency Contacts

These are the calls that need to happen in the first 30 minutes of any utility-related emergency. Make sure your on-island representative has all of these — and make sure they're listed on your accounts as an authorized contact.

Electricity

  • Hawaiian Electric (HECO) — O'ahu, Maui, Hawaii Island
    Outage reporting: (808) 548-7961
    Website: hawaiianelectric.com
    Note: HECO was acquired by a new parent company in 2024. Service numbers remain the same. Report downed lines as an emergency — do not approach.

Water

  • Board of Water Supply (BWS) — City & County of Honolulu
    24-hour emergency: (808) 748-5000
    Main water shutoff emergencies, broken mains, service line issues
    Tip: Know where your property's main water shutoff valve is located before an emergency. In older Kahala and Portlock homes, it's often near the meter at the street — not inside the house.

Gas

  • The Gas Company (TGC) — Hawaii's primary propane distributor
    Emergency line: (808) 526-0066
    If you smell gas: evacuate immediately, call from outside, do not use electrical switches

Sewer / Wastewater

  • Honolulu Department of Environmental Services (ENV)
    Sewer overflow / emergency: (808) 768-3777
    For properties with cesspool or septic systems (common in rural Ko'olau and North Shore areas): maintain a licensed cesspool pumping contractor separately.

Licensed Trade Contractors

This is the category most absentee owners get wrong. They either have no local contractors at all, or they have one general handyman who isn't licensed for the work that needs doing. Hawaii requires licensed contractors for most structural, electrical, plumbing, and mechanical work — unlicensed work voids insurance claims and creates liability.

Every property should have at minimum one vetted, licensed contact in each of the following categories:

Plumbing

Hawaii plumbing license (PB) is issued by the Hawaii DCCA. Verify any plumber's license at: pvl.ehawaii.gov

Priority use: water leaks, pipe failures, water heater emergencies, slab leaks. O'ahu's older homes — particularly in Kaimuki, Manoa, and Nuuanu — frequently have original cast iron or galvanized pipes that fail without warning.

Electrician

Keep a certified mold remediation company on file separately from your general contractor — they require specific IICRC certification and equipment.

Weather & Natural Disaster Resources

O'ahu's weather can be intense and fast-moving. Absentee owners should monitor these sources proactively — not reactively.

  • National Weather Service — Honolulu: weather.gov/hfo
    All official watches, warnings, and advisories for the Hawaiian Islands. Bookmark this.
  • Pacific Disaster Center: pdc.org
    Real-time disaster monitoring for the Pacific region.
  • Hawaii Emergency Management (Alert Hawaii): Sign up for emergency alerts at sms.ac/signup/hi
  • Honolulu Emergency Management: (808) 723-8960

Hurricane season: June 1 – November 30. Annual pre-season inspection recommended.
Flash flood season: November – March (windward side). Properties in Manoa, Kaneohe, Kailua, and Nuuanu are most exposed.
High surf season: November – February (North Shore). Properties on Ke Iki Rd, Sunset Beach, and Haleiwa should be inspected for coastal erosion and storm berm damage annually.

Frequently Asked Questions

What emergency contacts should every O'ahu property owner have?

Every O'ahu property owner should maintain contacts for: a licensed plumber, licensed electrician, HVAC technician, roofing contractor, general contractor, pest control company, locksmith, insurance agent, and a local estate or property manager. Absentee owners additionally need a trusted on-island contact who can be at the property within 30 minutes. Utility emergency lines — HECO, Board of Water Supply, The Gas Company — should be stored separately from your general contacts list so they're findable under stress.

Who do I call first if there's a water leak at my O'ahu home?

Your first call is your estate manager or local representative — they can physically respond and shut off the water at the main. If you don't have one, call a licensed plumber and simultaneously contact the Board of Water Supply at (808) 748-5000 if you suspect a supply line issue. Open your insurance claim immediately — do not wait to assess damage before calling. Delays in reporting can complicate claims in Hawaii.

How do I verify a Hawaii contractor's license?

All Hawaii contractor licenses can be verified through the DCCA Professional and Vocational Licensing portal at pvl.ehawaii.gov. Search by name or license number. Accepting work from an unlicensed contractor in Hawaii voids your homeowner's insurance coverage for that work and creates personal liability if anything goes wrong. This is not a technicality — it's enforced.

What should be stored with my on-site emergency contacts?

Along with your contact list, store: the location of the main water shutoff valve, the location and layout of your electrical panel with each breaker labeled, your insurance policy numbers and claims phone numbers, the combination or location of any on-site lockbox or safe, any alarm system codes or monitoring company information, and a simple floor plan showing utility shutoff locations. Update this documentation any time you make changes to the property.
