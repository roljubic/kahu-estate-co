Why Your Agreement Matters More Than the Fee

Most absentee owners shopping for property management on O'ahu focus on the headline fee — 8%, 10%, 12%. That number matters, but it's rarely where owners get hurt. The real exposure is in the contract.

A poorly written property management agreement can give your manager uncapped spending authority on your Kahala estate. It can lock you into a two-year relationship with a $5,000 exit penalty. It can leave you unprotected if the manager damages a tenant relationship or misses a permit deadline on your North Shore compound.

O'ahu property values are high. The stakes are correspondingly high. A $5M property in Portlock deserves an agreement reviewed with the same rigor you'd apply to any six-figure contract — because that's exactly what it is.

This guide walks through every section your agreement should contain, what fair language looks like, and what to push back on before you sign.

Scope of Services: Define Everything in Writing

The scope section is the most important part of any property management agreement. It defines what you're paying for — and what you're not. Vague scope language is the single most common source of disputes between O'ahu property owners and their managers.

What a Complete Scope Section Should Cover

For estate management (non-rental properties), your scope should specify:

  • Routine property inspections — frequency (monthly minimum), duration, documentation standard
  • Vendor management — who they coordinate, approval process, markup policy
  • Emergency response — response time commitment (24/7? Business hours only?)
  • Utility and bill management — what's handled on your behalf
  • Owner communication — format, frequency, response time
  • Pre-/post-storm protocols — especially important for properties in exposed areas like Lanikai, Kailua Beach, and North Shore
  • Access and key management — who has access, how it's logged

For rental management, add:

  • Tenant sourcing and screening standards
  • Lease preparation and execution
  • Rent collection and disbursement timeline
  • Maintenance request handling
  • Move-in/move-out inspections
  • Eviction authority (if any)

What "Full Service" Actually Means

Be skeptical of contracts that promise "full-service management" without enumerating what's included. In Hawaii, "full service" means something different to every manager. One firm's full service includes quarterly pest control and monthly pool service; another's covers only tenant-facing tasks. Get the list in writing.

Fee Structure — Every Line Item

A transparent fee schedule should leave no room for surprise invoices. Here's what should be explicitly stated:

Monthly Management Fee

Either a flat monthly retainer or a percentage of collected rent. For estate management (non-rental), flat retainers are standard — typically $2,500–$9,500/month for O'ahu luxury properties depending on property size and service tier. For rental management, percentage fees typically run 8–12% of gross collected rent.

Key question: Is the fee based on collected rent or scheduled rent? You should only pay on what's actually collected.

Leasing / Placement Fee

For rental properties, a one-time fee charged when a new tenant is placed — typically 50–100% of one month's rent. Confirm whether this is charged for renewals, and what happens if a tenant leaves within 90 days (many quality managers will waive or pro-rate re-leasing fees in that scenario).

Maintenance Markup

Some O'ahu managers mark up vendor invoices by 10–20%. Others pass costs through at cost. Neither is inherently wrong, but it must be disclosed in the agreement. A manager who marks up vendor work without disclosure is a serious red flag.

Ask specifically: "Do you charge a markup on maintenance and vendor invoices? If so, what percentage?"

Inspection Fees

Routine inspection fees should be included in your management fee. Some managers charge separately for move-in/move-out inspections ($75–$200), quarterly deep inspections, or storm inspections. These should be itemized, not buried.

Setup / Onboarding Fee

A reasonable one-time onboarding fee ($250–$750) for estate management is normal — it covers property intake, vendor setup, and account configuration. Onboarding fees above $1,000 warrant scrutiny.

Reserve Fund

Many managers require you to maintain a maintenance reserve — typically $500–$2,500 — held in their trust account. This is normal. Confirm: Is it your money? Does it earn interest? How is it returned at termination? What are the conditions under which they can draw from it?

Early Termination Fee

If you terminate before the contract end date, most agreements charge a fee — typically one to two months of management fees. This is standard. What's not standard: "tail fees" that require you to pay management fees on bookings that were made during your contract but check out after you've left. Read the exit clause carefully.

Manager Authority and Spend Limits

This section is where absentee owners are most exposed — and where the least attention is paid.

Pre-Approved Spend Limit

Your agreement must specify the dollar threshold below which the manager can authorize repairs or maintenance without contacting you. Standard for O'ahu luxury properties: $500–$750. Above that limit, they contact you for approval before proceeding.

If your agreement doesn't specify this limit, your manager has uncapped authority to charge repairs to your account. On a $5M estate in Diamond Head, that's an unacceptable exposure.

Vendor Selection Authority

Specify whether the manager can select vendors without your input, or whether you have approval rights on vendors above a certain spend threshold. Most owners are comfortable giving managers vendor discretion below the spend limit; above it, you may want the right to approve the vendor and the quote.

Emergency Authority

Carve out a clear emergency exception: in a genuine emergency (burst pipe, structural failure, post-hurricane damage), the manager should have authority to act immediately regardless of spend limit, with notification to you as soon as possible. Define what qualifies as an emergency in writing.

Reporting Requirements

As an absentee owner — whether you're in California, New York, Tokyo, or anywhere else — your agreement is your visibility into what's happening with your O'ahu property. Define it precisely.

Monthly Property Report

Your agreement should require a written monthly report that includes: inspection findings, vendor services performed, issues identified and resolved, upcoming scheduled maintenance, and a financial summary (expenses, reserve balance). Photos should be included — minimum 8 per inspection for a property of any size.

Response Time Commitments

What's the manager's committed response time to owner communications? 24 hours is standard for non-urgent items; 4 hours or less for urgent matters; immediate for emergencies. Get these committed in writing.

Financial Reporting

For rental properties: monthly income and expense statements, rent disbursement within a specified number of days after month-end (typically 10–15 business days), and annual 1099 filing. Your manager should use a dedicated trust account — co-mingling owner funds with operating funds is a violation of Hawaii real estate law.

Insurance and Liability

Manager's Insurance Requirements

Your agreement should confirm the manager carries:

  • General Liability: Minimum $1M per occurrence
  • Errors & Omissions (E&O): Covers mistakes, oversights, and professional negligence — critical for estate managers
  • Workers' Compensation: Required under Hawaii law if they employ staff

Request a certificate of insurance before signing and verify it's current. A manager who pushes back on this request is not a manager you want managing your property.

Property Insurance Responsibility

Clarify who is responsible for maintaining the property's own hazard insurance. Typically this remains with you (the owner), but the manager should be listed as an additional interested party on the policy so they're notified of any lapses.

Liability for Manager Actions

Understand the indemnification clause. Standard language protects the manager from liability for acts they performed in good faith within the scope of the agreement. What you want to avoid: indemnification language so broad that you're on the hook for the manager's negligence or misconduct.

Termination and Exit Clauses

The exit clause is the clause most owners don't read until they need it. Read it first.

Notice Period

Standard: 30–60 days written notice for either party to terminate. For estate management, 30 days is reasonable. For rental management with active tenants, 60 days gives time to transition without disrupting tenant relationships.

Termination for Cause

Your agreement should allow immediate termination (without notice period or penalty) if the manager: embezzles or misappropriates funds, grossly neglects the property, loses their Hawaii real estate license, or materially breaches the agreement. These protections are non-negotiable.

Transition Obligations

Upon termination, the manager should be required to: return all keys, access codes, and security devices; transfer all property records, vendor contacts, and documentation; provide a final accounting within 30 days; and return your maintenance reserve promptly. Specify a deadline (14–30 days) for the reserve return.

Post-Termination Obligations

Confirm there is no "tail period" — no obligation to pay management fees on future bookings or leases that were in place when you left. Some managers in O'ahu's vacation rental space insert tail clauses that survive termination; read carefully.

Hawaii-Specific Provisions

GET and TAT Compliance

Hawaii's General Excise Tax (GET) applies to property management fees. Your agreement should specify whether fees are quoted inclusive or exclusive of GET, and who is responsible for GET on vendor invoices. For vacation rental properties, your manager should handle Transient Accommodations Tax (TAT) filing — confirm this is explicitly included in scope.

Short-Term Rental Permit Compliance

If your property operates as a short-term rental, your agreement should include a provision requiring the manager to maintain permit compliance — including valid Nonconforming Use Certificate (NUC) or applicable permit, tax registration, and adherence to Honolulu's STR ordinance. A manager operating your property without proper permits exposes you to significant fines.

Hurricane and Storm Protocols

O'ahu properties — particularly those in Kailua, Lanikai, North Shore, and coastal Diamond Head — need specific hurricane protocols in the management agreement: pre-storm preparation steps, who coordinates with your insurer, post-storm inspection timelines, and emergency vendor authorization.

Governing Law

Your agreement should be governed by Hawaii law. Disputes should be resolved in the state courts of Hawaii (Honolulu/First Circuit) or through binding arbitration under Hawaii rules. Be cautious of agreements that designate an out-of-state jurisdiction.

Red Flags to Watch For

After reviewing hundreds of property management agreements across O'ahu, these are the provisions that should give you pause:

  • No pre-approved spend limit — your manager has uncapped authority over your maintenance budget
  • Automatic renewal without notice obligation — you can be locked in for another year if you miss a cancellation window
  • Maintenance markup not disclosed — you're paying inflated vendor invoices without knowing it
  • Fees charged on scheduled rent, not collected rent — you pay even when tenants don't
  • No termination for cause provision — you cannot exit even if the manager is negligent
  • Co-mingled trust accounts — your funds mixed with operating funds (illegal in Hawaii)
  • Tail clauses on existing bookings — you owe fees after you've terminated
  • Indemnification of manager negligence — you're liable for their mistakes
  • Vague "full service" scope without enumeration — anything not listed becomes a dispute
  • No reporting frequency specified — they can go months without sending a property update

None of these red flags are automatic dealbreakers — some can be negotiated out. But each one represents an exposure point you should understand before signing.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is a property management agreement legally required in Hawaii?

No law requires a written agreement, but any reputable manager will insist on one — and so should you. Without it, disputes over fees, authority, and liability become very difficult to resolve. Hawaii's real estate licensing laws (HRS Chapter 467) require licensed property managers to maintain written agreements for rental management services.

How long should a property management contract be on O'ahu?

Most O'ahu property management agreements run 12 months with automatic renewal. For estate management (non-rental), rolling 30- or 60-day agreements are more common and give owners more flexibility. Be cautious of any contract longer than 24 months without a clear early termination clause.

What fees should be itemized in a Hawaii property management agreement?

Your agreement should explicitly list: the monthly management fee, leasing/placement fees, maintenance markup (if any), inspection fees, onboarding/setup fees, advertising costs, reserve fund requirements, and early termination fees. Vague language like "additional services billed separately" is a red flag.

Can I terminate a property management agreement early in Hawaii?

Yes, but it depends on the contract. Most agreements allow early termination with 30–60 days notice and a termination fee (typically one to two months of management fees). Review the exit clause before signing — some O'ahu managers charge "tail fees" covering bookings made during your management period even after you leave.

What is the pre-approved spend limit in a property management agreement?

The pre-approved spend limit is the dollar threshold below which the manager can authorize repairs without contacting you. Standard on O'ahu is $300–$750. Above that limit, they must get your approval before proceeding. Make sure your agreement specifies this number clearly — if it doesn't, the manager has uncapped spending authority.

Should a property management agreement include insurance requirements?

Absolutely. Your agreement should specify the manager's required coverage: at minimum, general liability ($1M+) and errors & omissions (E&O). It should also clarify who maintains the property's hazard insurance and, for vacation rentals, what short-term rental coverage applies. Do not sign with a manager who cannot provide proof of current insurance.
