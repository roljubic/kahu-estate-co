How to Vet a Property Manager in Hawaii: The Questions Every O'ahu Owner Must Ask Before Signing

You own a property on O'ahu worth millions. You live somewhere else. Handing it to the wrong person is one of the most expensive mistakes an absentee owner can make — and unfortunately, it happens constantly. Here's how to make sure it doesn't happen to you.

Why This Matters More Than You Think

O'ahu has roughly 90,000 absentee property owners — mainland Americans, foreign nationals, and part-time residents who own high-value homes they can't personally oversee. Many of them eventually hire someone local to manage the property. Some of those stories end well. Many don't.

The problems that surface aren't usually dramatic at first. It's the slow drip: deferred maintenance that turns into a $40,000 repair. A vendor who was "handling everything" who wasn't. A guest who caused damage that was never properly documented. A reserve fund that somehow ran dry. By the time you find out, you're already deep in the hole.

The real estate and property management industry in Hawaii is large, loosely regulated in some corners, and includes a wide range from genuinely excellent operators to individuals who shouldn't be managing anyone's property. Knowing how to tell the difference before you sign anything is worth every minute of this read.

Step 1: Verify Their License Before You Meet

This is non-negotiable. In Hawaii, anyone who manages property for others — collecting rent, negotiating leases, placing tenants — generally needs a Hawaii real estate broker's license issued by the Department of Commerce and Consumer Affairs (DCCA).

The verification takes 30 seconds: go to pvl.ehawaii.gov, search their name or company name, and confirm the license is active and in good standing. Look for:

  • License type: should be Broker or Broker-in-Charge (not just Salesperson)
  • Status: Active (not Inactive, Suspended, or Expired)
  • Disciplinary actions: any complaints or formal sanctions on record

If they can't give you a license number, or if there's any history of complaints, the conversation is over before it begins.

Note: Estate managers who do not collect rent or negotiate leases may operate differently — but any company handling financial transactions related to your property should be able to clearly explain their licensing structure and how they handle the legal side of operations.

The 12 Questions to Ask in Your First Meeting

Don't wait for them to volunteer this information. Ask directly. A competent property manager will answer everything without hesitation. Vague answers, deflections, or "that's handled by our team" without specifics are yellow flags.

1. How many properties are you currently managing?

There's a sweet spot. A manager handling fewer than 10 properties may not have the systems you need. One handling 200+ properties may not give yours the attention it deserves. For luxury or estate properties in Kahala, Kailua, Ko Olina, or Portlock, you want someone who treats your property as a priority, not as unit 187 in a database.

2. What's the breakdown between luxury/high-value and standard properties?

A $7M estate in Lanikai has completely different needs from a $600K condo in Kaka'ako. Make sure their experience actually aligns with your property's class and complexity.

3. How do you handle after-hours emergencies?

Ask specifically: Who answers the phone at 2am if there's a burst pipe? What's the response protocol? Who are their emergency vendors? How fast can they get someone on-site? Vague answers here are a red flag — your property doesn't stop being at risk after 5pm.

4. What's your maintenance authorization threshold?

You need to know: how much can they spend on a repair without calling you first? $500? $2,500? $10,000? There's no universally right answer, but you need to agree on it upfront and get it in writing.

5. How are vendor relationships structured?

Some managers work with vendors who pay them referral fees or kickbacks. This isn't always disclosed. Ask point-blank: "Do any of your vendors pay you referral fees or commissions?" A trustworthy manager will answer clearly. One who gets evasive has told you something important.

6. What does your owner reporting look like?

Ask to see a sample report. How often do you get updates — weekly, monthly? What's included: maintenance logs, financial summaries, inspection reports, photos? For a luxury property, you should expect detailed monthly reporting and on-demand communication, not silence unless something breaks.

7. How do you handle property inspections?

How often? With photos? Using a standardized checklist? Who does them — the manager personally or a staff member? And critically: will you get copies?

8. What's your average response time to owner communications?

If you're calling from New York or Tokyo about your Kahala property, you need a manager who responds in hours, not days. Get their commitment on paper.

9. What insurance do you carry?

They should carry general liability and errors & omissions (E&O) insurance at minimum. Ask for a certificate of insurance before you sign anything.

10. What's your termination policy?

Can you exit the agreement with 30 days notice if things aren't working? Or are you locked in for 12 months? Beware of long lock-in periods with steep early termination fees. A confident manager doesn't need to trap you.

11. How do you handle security deposits and reserve funds?

Where are owner reserve funds held? In a separate trust account or commingled with operating funds? Hawaii law requires proper handling of trust funds — if they're vague about this, that's a serious red flag.

12. Can I speak with three current clients — specifically other absentee owners?

Not former clients. Current ones. And specifically people who don't live in Hawaii, because their experience mirrors yours most closely. Any manager worth hiring will provide these without hesitation.

7 Red Flags That Should End the Conversation

1. They can't produce a license number immediately

This is basic. If they fumble or say "we're working on it," walk away.

2. Fees significantly below market

If someone is offering to manage your $5M estate for $800/month when the market rate is $3,000–$6,000, the math doesn't work. Either they're cutting corners, using unqualified labor, or have a business model built around vendor kickbacks and markups you won't see until the invoices arrive.

3. Reluctance to provide client references

"Our clients prefer privacy" is not an acceptable answer. A trustworthy manager has happy clients who are willing to speak on their behalf. Full stop.

4. No written agreement — or an overly one-sided one

Any property manager who's casual about contracts is either inexperienced or hoping you won't notice what's not in writing. Read every line. Have an attorney review it for anything over six months or high-value properties.

5. Vague answers about vendor relationships

If they can't clearly explain how vendors are selected and compensated, assume there are financial relationships they don't want you to know about.

6. Slow or inconsistent communication during the sales process

If they're hard to reach before you've signed anything, imagine what it's like after. The way someone courts a client usually reflects their operating culture.

7. No dedicated point of contact

"Our team handles everything" means nobody is specifically accountable for your property. You want a named individual — someone you can call, someone who knows your property specifically, someone whose job it is to care about your asset.

What to Look for in the Management Agreement

The agreement is the operating system of the relationship. Here's what should be explicitly included:

  • Exact scope of services — itemized, not vague. "We handle everything" is not a scope of services.
  • Fee structure — flat monthly fee vs. percentage, and a complete list of any add-on charges (inspection fees, lease-up fees, maintenance markup, etc.)
  • Maintenance authorization limit — the dollar amount they can spend without your approval
  • Reporting schedule — how often, in what format, what's included
  • Termination terms — minimum 30-day mutual termination, no cause required
  • Insurance requirements — what the manager carries, what you're required to carry
  • Trust account provisions — how owner funds are held and disbursed
  • Dispute resolution — arbitration clause, governing law (Hawaii)

If any of these are missing or vague, request they be added before you sign. A professional property manager will have no objection to a clear contract.

How to Actually Check References

Most people call references, ask two surface-level questions, and accept whatever they hear. That's not a reference check — that's a formality. Here's how to actually use references to gather useful information:

Ask open-ended questions: "Tell me about a time something went wrong and how it was handled" is more revealing than "Are you happy with them?"

Ask about communication: "How quickly do they typically respond? Have you ever felt out of the loop?"

Ask about surprises: "Were there any fees or costs you didn't expect? Any gaps between what was promised and what was delivered?"

Ask the closing question: "If you were moving to a different property on O'ahu, would you hire them again?" A pause before "yes" is information.

You can also cross-reference: search the manager's company name + "reviews" on Google, Yelp, and the Better Business Bureau. Check Hawaii state court records at eCourt Kokua for any civil suits. This takes 20 minutes and has saved owners from very expensive mistakes.

Estate Management vs. Property Management: Which Do You Need?

For many high-value O'ahu properties — especially those in Kahala, Kailua's Lanikai and Beachside neighborhoods, Black Point, Portlock, and the North Shore's estate corridor — traditional property management isn't actually what the property needs.

Traditional property managers are designed around tenant placement and rent collection. They're optimized for occupancy and revenue, not for the care of a complex physical asset. If your O'ahu property is:

  • A primary or secondary residence (not an income-producing rental)
  • Worth $3M or more
  • Vacant for extended periods while you're on the mainland or abroad
  • A compound with multiple structures, pools, extensive landscaping, or smart home systems
  • A property you intend to pass down or eventually sell at peak value

…then what you need is estate management: a dedicated steward who treats your property as a physical asset to be preserved, maintained, and protected — not as a unit in a portfolio.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does a property manager in Hawaii need a real estate license?

Generally yes. If a property manager collects rent or negotiates leases on behalf of an owner, Hawaii law typically requires a real estate broker's license issued by the Hawaii DCCA. Always ask to see their license number and verify it at pvl.ehawaii.gov.

What is a reasonable property management fee in Hawaii?

For long-term rentals, expect 8–12% of monthly rent. For vacation rental management, fees typically run 20–30% of gross revenue. Estate management for luxury absentee-owner properties is usually a flat monthly retainer ranging from $2,500 to $12,000+ depending on property size and service scope. Anything significantly below market usually signals cut corners or hidden fees.

How do I check a property manager's track record in Hawaii?

Start with the DCCA license lookup at pvl.ehawaii.gov to confirm they're licensed and check for complaints. Then search the Hawaii State Judiciary's eCourt Kokua for any civil suits. Ask for three current client references — specifically other absentee owners, not local residents. And search their company name + "reviews" + "Hawaii" before any meeting.

What should be in a Hawaii property management agreement?

A solid agreement covers: exact scope of services, fee structure (flat vs. percentage, all-in vs. add-ons), maintenance authorization limits, reporting frequency, termination terms (look for 30-day mutual termination), insurance requirements, and how security deposits and reserve funds are held.

What's the difference between a property manager and an estate manager in Hawaii?

A property manager typically handles tenant placement and rent collection for income-producing properties. An estate manager manages the entire property as a physical asset: vendor coordination, preventive maintenance, emergency response, arrival preparation, and owner communication. Estate management is broader and more hands-on, designed for high-value properties where the owner is primarily absent.
